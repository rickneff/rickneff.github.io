Objective

To foster learning curiosity and learning permanence, to encourage inquisitiveness, and to spur application of the gersy principle.

Instructions

There are three parts to your submission:

  1. Create a connection between some topic from this week to previous class material, or anything relevant that you have learned before.
  2. Create a question that shows genuine curiosity — not just trying to “demonstrate rich and deep comprehension of the subject”. (That focus has tended to produce complicated and complex questions that are difficult to unpack and understand.)
  3. Create a GPAO that you think would be fun and/or interesting to explore.

Create a GPAO

Regarding creativity in general, and GPAOs in particular:

Steve Olson and Paul Zietz are two kindred spirits. Check out the below excerpt from Steve Olson’s book where he introduces Paul Zietz and quotes him on his approach to teaching creative problem solving, which Paul Zietz believes can be taught — but his approach may surprise you!

Paul Zeitz, now a professor of mathematics at the University of San Francisco, was a member of the U.S. Olympiad team that competed [in 1974].

[…]

Zeitz [is] always interested in applying the problem solving he’d done as an Olympian in his teaching. So he [developed] a series of tough mathematical challenges that his students would work on for extended periods. He found this approach often engaged the least talented kids just as much as the most talented.

[…]

He [wrote the book] The Art and Craft of Problem Solving, which was [first] published in 1999. It’s a challenging book — many math Olympians use it to prepare for the competition. Zeitz uses it with all the students in his classes — including those studying to be teachers — often by breaking them up into teams and letting them work on specific problems for days at a time. “The level of the math is not really an issue,” he says. “My main goal when I teach this kind of course is to inspire my students to really think hard. It sounds simplistic, but that’s all there is to it. I want to get students used to a mindset that a problem that is worth solving is worth pondering for a long time. It’s hard to train somebody how to think for a long amount of time. Most people think in maybe five-second bursts. I’m talking about spending days thinking about something. So the level of the math is less an issue than is rewiring their brains so they can concentrate better.”

Rewiring the brains of students is an ambitious goal, Zeitz admits. But it can be done by demonstrating that math is not what people think it is. “To be good at math you have to be both rigorous and a good explorer,” he says. “And this exploration, this organized play, that’s what people don’t understand about math — it’s a very social activity.”

Pursuing GPAOs is an excellent opportunity for this social activity of problem exploration. And yes, creating GPAOs (not just pursuing them) is good for demonstrating “A”-student-like above and beyond behavior. Who to team up with, to learn from and teach one another — you decide, and if you do collaborate on the creation of GPAOs (which you are strongly encouraged to do) mention that fact (but no names, for anonymity’s sake) when you post your submission on Kritik.

(defun build-prime-prod (max last-checked product)
  (if (>= last-checked max)
      product
    (let* ((to-check (1+ last-checked)))
      (build-prime-prod max to-check (if (= (gcd product to-check) 1)
                                         (* product to-check)
                                       product))))))

(defun is-prime (x)
  (let ((product (build-prime-prod (sqrt x) 1 1)))
    (= (gcd x product) 1)))

(mapcar (lambda (x) (list x (is-prime x))) (number-sequence 2 256))
