Read and Heed

The most important advice I can give you is to pray to know what grade you should claim. That is the same advice as last time. You and your Heavenly Father know which of the grade descriptions spelled out in the syllabus best describes you. I, however, need to be convinced with evidence!

So one more time, please reread the syllabus where it talks about grading. Then

  • Double check that your Course Tracker table is complete, with the grade you will claim decided beforehand. (Our 5 minutes together must be free of fumbling, hemming or hawing, or wishy-washiness of any kind. In other words, do your soul-searching before, not during our meeting!)
  • Come prepared with laptop charged and spacemacs and browser open.
  • Have a tab holding the exported-to-html work of each of the last 5 weeks (5 tabs total).
  • Come prepared to discuss your WOK, including what you understand about RSA and one other topic I will choose.

Important For A-Grade Claimers

If you aspire to and think you should claim an A, please heed the following:

You must only check the ABC column for each week if your record of “Above and Beyond Climbing” shows complete compliance with what the syllabus says — with one change: where the syllabus says each week throughout each 4-week period, change that to say each of no fewer than 4 of the last 5 weeks, as in:

To claim [an A] you must have done and recorded evidence in your portfolio of doing [at least two of the first three and at least two of the last three] behaviors listed below, each of no fewer than 4 of the last 5 weeks.

  1. teaching and/or helping others in the class but not in your group;
  2. teaching a Non-CS, Non-CE, Non-EE, Non-SE major about the material in this class to help them with a class they are taking;
  3. applying what you’ve learned in this class in another class you are currently taking;
  4. posting thoughtful questions;
  5. solving puzzles (see above) or problems marked as optional; and
  6. doing other work not assigned, exploring other topics in mathematics, writing code implementing what you are learning that has not been assigned, etc.

A High Bar!

It is a high bar — which it should be.

You may be thinking, “that’s too much to expect — and since I can’t claim an A- (which is more reflective of reality), is a B really the best I can get?”

Replace that thought with this one: “My integrity is more important than a grade!”

You may be thinking, “this way of grading seems to encourage procrastination of all-out effort — like I can just cruise along until the last 2 or 3 weeks and then floor it and still claim an A in the end. Applying the principle of repentance!”

Replace that thought with: “Repentance is a true principle, but deathbed repentance is not. Even though only the last grade claim counts (quote-unquote), the parable of the workers in the vineyard does not apply.”

Are You a Label Learner?!

To handle problems requiring data beyond the capacity of students and educators impatient to shine, the ancient Sophists devised certain very effective discussion techniques. In these, the most important skill was that of presenting evidence by implication or inference only. Since it is quite impossible in a public discourse (or in print, for that matter) to put all one’s evidence on display, one must be allowed on occasion to present one’s knowledge merely by inference. The Sophists seized upon this welcome path of escape from drudgery, and by the arts of rhetoric made of it a broad highway to successful teaching careers. A limited use of jargon is indispensable in any field: having solved for “x”, we do not have to derive “x” every time it is mentioned, but simply to indicate it by a symbol, such as those useful keywords commonly used to power historical discussions: The Medieval Mind, Sturm und Drang, the Frontier, Hellenism, the Enlightenment, Puritanism, the Primitive, Relativity, etc., each of which is supposed to set a whole chorus of bells chiming in our heads—the echoes of deep and thorough reading. But by a more familiar process these labels are no mere labels anymore; they have become the whole substance of our knowledge. The student today has never solved for that “x” about which he talks so glibly—he has got its value from an answerbook; the cue word is not just a cue; it is now the whole play.

(Hugh Nibley, Do Religion and History Conflict?, Temple and Cosmos, The Collected Works of Hugh Nibley: Volume 12 Ancient History, 442)

Words From a Professor

Most of us know what we know. Fewer know what they don’t know. All of us don’t know as much as we need to know in order to enjoy productive, refining thinking. Yet we all tend to resist new knowledge, content in the truth that what we already know is all we really need to know to get by. Gaining greater knowledge, then, requires us to fight this tendency to live to get by. But when getting by seems good enough, how does one develop the desire to learn to change?

If embraced as an excuse for not learning what one ought to learn, “ignorance” is a word that doesn’t refer primarily to the absence of understanding. Rather it refers to a fundamental commitment to ignore that which challenges one’s comfortable thinking. To admit that one is ignorant in this regard is not to confess that one doesn’t understand. Rather it is to confess that one chooses not to understand. (David L. Ward)

An Analogy to Ponder

Learning is like eating — but the menu is not the meal!

Like a Label Learner, a person who goes to a restaurant, looks over the full-color menu showing a dazzling array of appetizing dishes, salivates in anticipation, and then walks out — leaves very unsatisfied.

Don’t be that person!
